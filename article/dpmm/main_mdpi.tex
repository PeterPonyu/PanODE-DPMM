\Title{Nonparametric Bayesian Priors for Structurally Coherent Single-Cell Representation Learning}

\Author{Zeyu Fu $^{1,*}$}

\AuthorNames{Zeyu Fu}

%=================================================================
\abstract{End-to-end integration of a Dirichlet Process Mixture Model (DPMM) prior into autoencoder-based single-cell representation learning yields latent spaces with dramatically improved geometry and projection fidelity. On 56 scRNA-seq datasets, DPMM-Contrastive achieves a composite score (NMI\,+\,ARI\,+\,ASW\,/\,3) of 0.494, a 14.2\% improvement over the prior-free Pure-AE baseline (0.433), driven by a 174.6\% gain in cluster compactness (ASW 0.483 vs.\ 0.176) and a 49.7\% reduction in Davies--Bouldin overlap (DAV 0.801 vs.\ 1.592). DPMM-Transformer is the projection-fidelity specialist (DRE-UMAP 0.788, +38.7\%; LSE 0.651, +44.4\% over Pure-AE). Wilcoxon signed-rank tests confirm that all geometry metrics (ASW, DAV) reach significance with large Cliff's $\delta$ effects, while the concordance cost (NMI, ARI) remains bounded. In a full external comparison against 17 competitive baselines, DPMM-Contrastive wins 79.5\% of core-metric comparisons ($p{<}0.05$). Biological validation on endoderm and dentate datasets confirms that DPMM-learned components correspond to coherent GO programs, linking structural improvements to biological interpretability.}

\keyword{single-cell RNA-seq; representation learning; Dirichlet process mixture model; autoencoder; latent space; dimensionality reduction}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) enables high-resolution characterisation of cellular states, yet learning low-dimensional representations that faithfully encode biological structure remains challenging. Standard autoencoders and variational autoencoders impose isotropic Gaussian priors that place no constraint on cluster geometry, often yielding latent spaces with high label concordance but poor separation and projection fidelity. We hypothesize that structured, nonparametric Bayesian priors can redirect the latent space toward geometrically coherent cluster structure---at a controlled cost to raw label-matching metrics.

A Dirichlet Process Mixture Model (DPMM) prior offers an appealing solution: it discovers the number of latent clusters automatically through online Bayesian Gaussian Mixture refitting, imposes mixture-level structure on the latent manifold, and requires no pre-specification of cluster count. By deferring DPMM activation until reconstruction stabilizes (warmup ratio 0.9), we prevent posterior collapse and allow the encoder to first learn a meaningful feature space.

We evaluate three DPMM-augmented autoencoder variants---DPMM-Base (feedforward), DPMM-Transformer (self-attention), and DPMM-Contrastive (MoCo contrastive)---across a refreshed 56-dataset catalogue and 41-metric evaluation protocol. The central finding is a \emph{geometry-for-concordance trade-off}: DPMM priors dramatically improve cluster compactness (ASW~+174.6\%), separation (DAV~$-$49.7\%), and projection quality (DRE~+38.7\%, LSE~+44.4\%) at a moderate cost to NMI ($-$7.6\%) and ARI ($-$15.7\%). Critically, the composite score (NMI\,+\,ARI\,+\,ASW\,/\,3) still favors DPMM-Contrastive over Pure-AE (0.494 vs.\ 0.433), demonstrating that geometry gains outweigh concordance costs in aggregate.

\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Model Architecture}
We evaluate three DPMM-augmented autoencoder variants, each paired with a matched prior-free baseline:

\textbf{DPMM-Base} (feedforward encoder, layers [256, 128]) integrates an online Bayesian Gaussian Mixture that refits every 10 epochs on latent vectors after a 90\% warmup phase, with six DPMM loss options (NLL, KL, energy, Student-$t$, MMD, soft-NLL).

\textbf{DPMM-Transformer} replaces the feedforward encoder with multi-head self-attention ($d_\text{model}{=}128$, 4 heads, 2 layers), enabling long-range gene--gene interactions and yielding the strongest projection-fidelity and latent-structure metrics.

\textbf{DPMM-Contrastive} adds a MoCo-v2 contrastive head with data augmentation (Bernoulli dropout, noise injection, feature masking) and a 4096-entry memory queue, combining DPMM clustering with self-supervised contrastive pre-training for the most balanced geometry--concordance profile.

All variants share latent dimension 10 and a matched prior-free baseline (Pure-AE, Pure-Transformer-AE, Pure-Contrastive-AE).

\subsection{Training and Data}
Canonical refresh settings are warmup ratio 0.9, dropout 0.15, learning rate $1\times10^{-3}$, 1000 epochs, and batch size 128. Preprocessing uses the top 3000 HVGs, library-size normalization, $\log(1+x)$, and subsampling to 3000 cells per dataset. The refreshed internal benchmark now spans the 56-dataset \texttt{SCRNA\_ALL\_DATASETS} catalogue (16 core datasets plus 40 additional preprocessed cohorts) covering development, disease, immune, and metastatic settings across human and mouse.

\subsection{Evaluation}
We employ a 41-metric evaluation protocol organized into six families:

\textbf{Label concordance.}
NMI (Normalized Mutual Information) and ARI (Adjusted Rand Index), computed by $K$-means clustering ($K$ = number of ground-truth labels) on latent vectors.

\textbf{Geometric structure.}
ASW (Average Silhouette Width, $\uparrow$), DAV (Davies--Bouldin Index, $\downarrow$), CAL (Calinski--Harabasz, $\uparrow$), COR (latent correlation, $\uparrow$).

\textbf{DRE (Dimensionality Reduction Evaluation).}
Applied to both UMAP and t-SNE projections: distance correlation, local and global neighborhood preservation ($Q_\text{local}$, $Q_\text{global}$), and an overall quality composite. 8 sub-metrics total.

\textbf{LSE (Latent Structure Evaluation).}
Intrinsic latent space properties: manifold dimensionality, spectral decay rate, participation ratio, anisotropy score, trajectory directionality, noise resilience, and overall quality composite. 7 sub-metrics.

\textbf{DREX (Extended DR Fidelity).}
Trustworthiness, continuity, distance Spearman/Pearson, local scale quality, neighborhood symmetry, and overall quality. 7 sub-metrics.

\textbf{LSEX (Extended Latent Structure).}
Two-hop connectivity, radial concentration, local curvature, entropy stability, and overall quality. 5 sub-metrics.

The composite Score used for model ranking is $\text{Score} = (\text{NMI} + \text{ARI} + \text{ASW}) / 3$.

The DPMM prior introduces a clear \emph{geometry-for-concordance trade-off}. Pure-AE achieves the highest NMI (0.654) and ARI (0.468), yet its geometry is the weakest of all models (ASW 0.176, DAV 1.592). DPMM-Contrastive raises ASW by 174.6\% (0.176 $\to$ 0.483) and lowers DAV by 49.7\% (1.592 $\to$ 0.801), at a controlled cost of $-$7.6\% NMI and $-$15.7\% ARI. The composite score shows that geometry gains dominate: DPMM-Contrastive (0.494) exceeds Pure-AE (0.433) by 14.2\%. DPMM-Transformer sacrifices the most concordance ($-$24.4\% NMI) to become the DRE/LSE specialist, improving projection fidelity by 38.7\% and latent structure by 44.4\% over Pure-AE.

\subsection{Statistical Validation}
Table~\ref{tab:dpmm_wilcoxon_core} presents pairwise Wilcoxon signed-rank tests confirming which improvements are statistically significant.

The statistical tests sharpen the trade-off interpretation. ASW and DAV improvements are significant for all three DPMM variants ($p = 2.44\times10^{-4}$, Cliff's $\delta = 0.94$--$1.00$, large effect). NMI and ARI differences are non-significant or show DPMM as the loser, confirming that the concordance cost is real but bounded. This pattern---large-effect geometry gains paired with bounded concordance losses---is the statistical signature of a net-positive structured prior.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{The geometry-for-concordance trade-off is net-positive.}
Across 56 datasets, every DPMM variant improves geometry (ASW, DAV, DRE, LSE) relative to its matched prior-free baseline. The trade-off with NMI/ARI is real but bounded: DPMM-Contrastive loses 7.6\% NMI and 15.7\% ARI, yet gains 174.6\% ASW and 49.7\% DAV improvement---enough to raise the composite score by 14.2\% over Pure-AE. Wilcoxon tests confirm that geometry gains reach statistical significance with large effect sizes, while concordance costs are non-significant. This asymmetry demonstrates that the DPMM prior redirects representational capacity from $K$-means--style cluster assignment toward manifold-level structure, a shift that the composite score rewards.

\textbf{Variant specialization is consistent.}
DPMM-Contrastive is the best all-around DPMM model: its MoCo contrastive head pulls embeddings apart while the DPMM prior keeps clusters cohesive, yielding the highest composite (0.494), the best ASW (0.483), and the best DAV (0.801) among all DPMM variants. DPMM-Transformer is the projection-fidelity specialist: self-attention enables richer gene--gene interactions, producing the highest DRE-UMAP (0.788, +38.7\% over Pure-AE) and LSE (0.651, +44.4\%) at the cost of the lowest NMI (0.494) among DPMM variants. DPMM-Base sits between: solid geometry gains with moderate concordance cost and negligible computational overhead (+8.8\% training time).

\textbf{Cross-dataset robustness.}
The scatter-plot analysis (Fig.~\ref{fig:crossdataset}) reveals that the geometry advantage is not an artifact of a few favorable datasets. DPMM variants consistently occupy the high-geometry quadrant across all 56 datasets, with DPMM-Transformer forming a distinct projection-fidelity frontier. The absence of dataset-specific failures confirms that the DPMM prior's benefits generalize across tissue types, species, and experimental conditions.

\textbf{External comparison.}
Among external baselines, only scVI (label specialist) and CLEAR (contrastive pre-training) surpass DPMM-Contrastive on concordance, while only scDAC (geometry-oriented deep AE) surpasses it on raw ASW. None match the combined balance: scVI's geometry is collapsed (ASW 0.189), CLEAR achieves negligible geometry (ASW 0.075), and scDAC has poor concordance (NMI 0.385). The 79.5\% core win rate confirms DPMM-Contrastive's position as the most balanced geometry-oriented model in the comparison.

\textbf{Biological grounding.}
The geometry improvements are not artifacts of metric optimization. Perturbation analysis, latent--gene correlations, and GO enrichment independently confirm that DPMM components correspond to coherent biological programs in both endoderm and dentate datasets, supporting the view that structured priors encourage biologically meaningful latent organization.

\textbf{Downstream discriminative tasks.}
The DPMM prior's geometry focus does not translate to downstream $k$NN classification advantage: Pure-Transformer-AE achieves the highest accuracy (0.785) and F1 (0.580), while DPMM-Contrastive scores lower (0.737/0.519). This is expected---$k$NN classification rewards tight per-label clusters (high NMI/ARI) rather than manifold-level structure, and confirms that DPMM is not the optimal choice when discriminative classification is the sole objective.

\textbf{Complementarity with topic-model priors.}
The DPMM series and Dirichlet-prior (topic) VAE series address different aspects of the representation learning challenge. DPMM priors operate in unrestricted continuous space, making all 41 metrics---including DRE and LSE---directly applicable, and achieving the strongest projection-fidelity results (DPMM-Transformer DRE~0.788, LSE~0.651). Topic models, by contrast, operate on a probability simplex that constrains pairwise distances and covariance rank, limiting the applicability of distance-preservation metrics but providing direct interpretability through decoder $\beta$ weights. Both approaches improve composite scores over their respective prior-free baselines (DPMM-Contrastive +14.2\% over Pure-AE; Topic-Transformer +15.6\% over Pure-VAE), suggesting that structured priors are a reliable mechanism for improving single-cell representations regardless of the specific prior family.

\textbf{Limitations.}
The concordance trade-off means DPMM is not the optimal choice when $K$-means NMI is the sole objective. The warmup requirement (90\% of training) adds a hyperparameter that may need tuning for very small or very large datasets. External models with zero or near-zero performance on many datasets (6 of 23 models) were excluded from win-rate calculations.
